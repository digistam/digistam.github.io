Hacking OT

Operational technology (OT) plays a major role in today's modern society, as it drives a collection of devices designed to work together as an integrated or homogeneous system. OT is a combination of hardware and software that is used to monitor, run and control industrial process assets. These devices include switches, pumps, lights, sensors, surveillance cameras, elevators, robots, valves, cooling and heating systems. Any system that analyzes and processes operational data can be part of OT.

      Industrial Control Systems (ICSs)
      Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA)
      Remote Terminal Units (RTU)
      Programmable Logic Controllers (PLC)
      Distributed Control Systems (DCS)

Essential Terminology

      Assets -> different components of OT are generally referred to as assets
      Zones and Conduits -> network segregation technique used to isolate networks and assets to impose and maintain strong access control mechanisms
      Industrial Network and Business Network -> collection of automated control systems
      Industrial Protocols -> S7, CDA, SRTP, Modbus, OPC, DNP3, CIP etc.
      Network Perimeter / Electronic Security Perimeter -> outermost boundary of a network zone (closed group of assets)
      Critical Infrastructure -> collection of physical or logical systems and assets, the failure or destruction will severly impact security, safety, economy or public healts

IT/OT Convergence (IIOT)

IT/OT convergence is the integration of IT computing systems and OT operation monitoring systems. Bridging the gap between IT and OT can improve the overall business, producing faster and efficient results. IT and OT teams are traditionally separated and are found in their respective domains. For instance, IT teams monitor internal processes and safeguard networks from cyber-attacks, whereas OT teams ensure overall maintenance and management, including that of employees and industrial equipment.

Purdue Model

Derived from the Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture (PERA) model, which is a widely used conceptual model that describes the internal connections and dependencies of important components in ICS networks. The Purdue Model consists of three zones:

      Manufacturing Zone (OT)
      Enterprise Zone (IT)
      Demilitarized Zone (DMZ)

The Enterprise security zone is part of IT, in which supply-chain management and scheduling are performed using business systems such as SAP and ERP. It also locates the data centers, users and cloud access. It has two levels:

      Level 5 (Enterprise Network) -> B2B, B2C services, internet connectivity, inventory etc.
      Level 4 (Business Logistics Systems) -> all the IT systems supporting the production process in the plant lie at this level

The Manufacturing Zone contains the OT systems. All the devices, networks, control and monitoring systems reside in this zone. Four levels:

      Level 3 (Operational Systems / Site Operations)
      Level 2 (Control Systems / Area Supervisory Controls)
      Level 1 (Basic Controls / Intelligent Devices)
      Level 0 (Physical Process)

Industrial Control System (ICS)

ICS is an essential part of every industrial process and critical infrastructure. A typical ICS represents the information system that controls and supports all types of industrial processes, such as production, manufacturing, product handling, distribution etc. An ICS comprises several types of control systems, such as SCADA, DCS, BPC, Safety Instrumentation Systems (SIS), HMI etc. This technology consists of various components such as sensors, controllers and actuators (mechanical, electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic etc.) that act collectively to achieve and industrial objective. The operation of ICS can be configured in three modes:

      Open Loop (output of system depends on the preconfigured settings)
      Closed Loop (output always has an effect on the input to acquire the desired objective)
      Manual Loop (system totally under control of humans)

Components of an ICS

      Distributed Control System (DCS) -> highly engineered and large-scale control system used to perform industry-specific task
      Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) -> centralized supervisory control system used for controlling and monitoring industrial facilities and infrastructure
      Programmable Logic Controller (PLC) -> digital solid-state computer used for industrial automation, built to survive severe industrial environments
      Basic Process Control System (BPCS) -> system that responds to input signals from processes and associated equipment; act as first layer of protection agains any unsafe or hazardous condition to the equipment
      Safety Instrumented Systems (SIS) -> automated control system designed to safeguard the manufacturing environment in case of any hazardous incident; it consists of sensors, logic solvers and final control elements

OT Technologies and Protocols

The key communication technologies and protocols of the OT network over the Purdue model are as follows:

      Level 4, 5 (DCOM, DDE, FTP/SFTP, GE-SRTP, IPv4/IPv6, OPC, TCP/IP, WiFi)
      Level 3 (CC-Link, DDE, GE-SRTP, HSCP, ICCP, IEC 61850, ISA/IEC 62443), Modbus, NTP, Profinet, SuiteLink, Tase-2, TCP/IP)
      Level 2 (6LoWPAN, CC-Link, DNP3, DNS/DNSSEC, FTE, HART-IP, IEC 60870-5-101/104, IPv4/IPv6, ISA/IEC 62443, OPC, NTP, SOAP, TCP/IP)
      Level 0, 1 (BACnet, EtherCat, CANopen, Crimson v3, DeviceNet, GE-SRTP, Zigbee, ISA/IEC 62443, ISA SP100, MELSEC-Q, Modbus, Niagara Fox, Omron Fins, PCWorx, Profibus, Profinet, Sercos II, S7 Communications, WiMAx)

Protocols explained

      DCOM: Distributed Component Object Model, Microsoft's proprietary software that enables software components to communicate directly over a network reliably and securely
      DDE: Dynamic Data Exchange used for Inter-Process Communication (IPC)
      GE-SRTP: Service Request Transport Protocol, developed by GE Intelligent Platforms, used to transfer data from PLCs
      OPC: Open Platform Communications, set of client/server protocols designed for the communication of real-time data between data acquisition devices like PLCs
      CC-Link: Control and Communications Link, enables devices from different manufactures to communicate
      HSCP: Hybrid SCP (Secury Copy Protocol)
      ICCP (IEC 60870-6): Inter-Control Center Communications Protocol, provides a set of standards and protocols for covering ICS or SCADA communication in power system automation
      IEC 61850: common protocol that enables interoperability and communications between IEDs
      ISA/IEC 62443: flexible framework for addressing and mitigating current and future security vulnerabilities
      Modbus: serial communication protocol used with PLCs and enables communication between many devices
      Profinet: communication protocol used to exchange data between controllers like PLCs and devices like RFID readers
      SuiteLink: based on TCP/IP and runs as a service on Windows operating systems
      Tase-2: open communication protocol that enables the exchange of time-critical information between control systems through WAN and LAN
      DNP3: Distributed Network Protocol 3, communication protocol used to interconnect components within process automation systems
      FTE: Fault Tolerant Ethernet, designed to provide rapid network redundancy, each node connected twice to a single LAN
      HART-IP: integrate WirelessHART gateways and HART multiplexers tightly and efficiently for sending and receiving digital information
      IEC 60870-5-101/104: extension of IEC 101 protocol
      BACnet: Building Automation and Control Network, data communication protocol designed for building automation and control networks
      EtherCAT: Ethernet for Control Automation Technology, Ethernet based fieldbus system
      CANopen: high level communication protocol based on CAN protocol, used for embedding networking applications like vehicle networks
      Crimson: common programming platform
      DeviceNet: variant of Common Industrial Protocol (CIP)
      ISA SP100: committee for establishing the industrial wireless standard ISA100
      MELSEC-Q: provides open and seamless network environment integrating different levels of automation networks such as CC-Link
      Niagara Fox: building automation protocol
      Omron Fins: used by PLC programs for transferring data and performing other services with remote PLC connected on Ethernet
      PCWorx: used in many ICS components, make series of inline controllers (ILCs)
      Profibus: designed and developed to address interoperability issues
      Sercos II: serial real-time communication system, digital drive interface
      S7 Communication: Siemens proprietary protocol that runs between PLCs of Siemens
      WiMax: Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access (WiMax)

      Unrestricted outbound internet access from OT networks

Information Security Concepts

Information security: protection or safeguarding of information and information systems that use, store and transmit information from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration and destruction. Information is a critical asset that organizations must secure.

Elements of information security

      Confidentiality
      Integrity
      Availability
      Authenticity
      Non-repudiation

It's important to remember that CIA is for data and the last two (Authenticity, Non-repudiation) is for users !

Motives, goals and objectives of information security attacks

Attackers generally have motives (goals) and objectives behind their information security attacks. Once the attacker determines their goal, they can employ various tools, attack techniques and methods to exploit vulnerabilities in a computer system or security policy and controls

Attacks = Motive (Goal) + Method + Vulnerability

It's important to remember that without one of the elements (motive, method, vulnerability) there will be no attack !

Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs)

TTPs refer to the patterns of activities and methods associated with specific threat actors or groups of threat actors. Organizations should understand TTPs to protect their network against threat actors and upcoming attacks.

Tactics

Tactics is defined as a strategy followed by an attacker to perform the attack from the beginning to the end.

Techniques

Techniques is defined as the technizal methods used by an attacker to achieve results during the attack.

Procedures

Procedures is defined as a systematic approach followed by the threat actor to launch an attack.

In order to understand and defend against threat actors, it is important to understand the TTPs used by adversaries. Understanding the tactics of an attacker helps to predict and detect evolving threats in the early stages. Understanding the techniques used by attackers helps to identify vulnerabilities and implement defensive measures. Analyzing the procedures used by attackers helps to identify what the attacker is looking for within the target organization's infrastructure.

Vulnerabilities

A vulnerability refers to a weakness in the design or implementation of a system that can be exploited to compromise the security of the system. It is frequently a security loophole that enables an attacker to enter the system by bypassing user authentication. There are generally two main causes for vulnerable systems in a network:

      software or hardware misconfiguration
      poor programming practices

Common reasons for the existence of vulnerabilities

      Hardware or software misconfiguration
      Insecure or poor design of network and application
      Inherent technology weaknesses
      End-user carelessness
      Intentional end-user acts

Classification of attacks

      Passive attacks -> footprinting, sniffing, eavesdropping, network traffic analysis, decryption of weakly encrypted traffic
      Active attacks -> DoS, bypassing protection mechanisms, malware attacks (virus, worm, ransomware), modification of information, spoofing attacks, replay attacks etc.
      Close-in attacks -> social engineering (shoulder surfing, dumpster diving etc.)
      Insider attacks -> eavesdropping, wiretapping, theft of physical devices, data theft, pod slurping, planting keyloggers, backdoors, malware
      Distribution attacks -> tampering with hardware or software prior to installation

Information Warfare

Information warfare (InfoWar): use of information and communication technologies (ICT) for competitive advantages over an opponent

      Command and control warfare (C2 warfare) -> impact an attacker possesses over a compromised system or network that they control
      Intelligence based warfare -> sensor-based technology that directly corrupts technological systems
      Electronic warfare -> radio electronic and cryptographic techniques to degrade communication
      Psychological warfare -> propaganda, terror etc.
      Hacker warfare -> all things digital
      Economic warfare -> affecting economy
      Cyberwarfare -> use of information systems against virtual personas of individuals or groups

Each form of information warfare mentioned above consists of both defensive and offensive strategies.

      Defensive Information Warfare -> all strategies and actions to defend against attacks on ICT assets
      Offensive Information Warfare -> involves attacks against the ICT assets of an opponent

Hacking Concepts

Hacking in the field of computer security refers to exploiting system vulnerabilities and compromising security controls to gain unauthorized or inappropriate access to system resources. It involves a modifying system or application features to achieve a goal outside its creator's original purpose.

A hacker is a person who breaks into a system or network without authorization to destroy, steal sensitive data, or perform malicious attacks.

Ethical Hacking Concepts

Ethical hacking is the practice of employing computer and network skills in order to assist organizations in testing their network security for possible loopholes and vulnerabilities.

Ethical hacking is necessary as it allows to counter attacks from malicious hackers by anticipating methods used by them to break into a system. Ethical hacking helps to predict various possible vulnerabilities well in advance and rectify them without incurring any kind of outside attack.

An ethical hacker's evaluation of a client's information system security seeks to answer three basic questions:

      What can an attacker seen on the target system
      What can an intruder do with that information
      Are the attacker's attempts being noticed on the target system

Ethical hacking is a structured and organized security assessment, usually as part of a penetration test or security audit, and is a crucial component of risk assessment.

Hacking Methodologies and Frameworks

CEH Hacking Methodology (CHM)

EC council's CEH hacking methodology (CHM) defines the step-by-step process to perform ethical hacking. The CHM follows the same process as that of an attacker, and the only differences are in its hacking goals and strategies.

An understanding of the CEH ethical hacking framework helps ethical hackers learn various tactics, techniques and tools used by attackers at various phases of hacking.

      Phase 1: Reconnaissance
      Phase 2: Vulnerability scanning
      Phase 3: Gaining access
      Phase 4: Maintaining access
      Phase 5: Clearing tracks

The following steps take place in theses phases:

      Phase 1: Reconnaissance
      Footprinting
      Scanning -> identify active hosts, open ports and unnecessary services enabled on particular hosts
      Enumeration -> making active connections to a target system or subjecting it to direct queries; method of intrusive probing trough which attackers gather information
      Phase 2: Vulnerability scanning
      Vulnerability analysis
      Phase 3: Gaining access
      System hacking -> gaining access, escalating privileges
      Phase 4: Maintaining access
      maintaining access
      Phase 5: Clearing Tracks
      clearing tracks

Cyber Kill Chain Methodology

The cyber kill chain methodology is a framework developed for securing cyberspace based on the concept of military kill chains. This method aims to actively enhance intrusion detection and response. The cyber kill chain is equipped with a seven - phase protection mechanism to mitigate and reduce cyber threats.

      Reconnaissance -> gather data on the target to probe for weak points
      Weaponization -> create a deliverable malicious payload using exploit and backdoor
      Delivery -> send weaponized bundle to the victim using email, USB etc.
      Exploitation -> exploit a vulnerability by executing code on the victim's system
      Installation -> install malware on the target system
      Command and Control -> create C2 channel to communicate and pass data back and forth
      Actions on Objectives -> perform actions to achieve goals and objectives

Adversary Behavioral Identification

Adversary behavioral identification involves the identification of the common methods or techniques followed by an adversary to launch attacks to penetrate an organization's network. This gives security professionals insight into upcoming threats and exploits. It helps them plan network security infrastructure and adapt a range of security procedures as prevention against various attacks.

      Internal Reconnaissance: Once the adversary is inside the target network, they follow various techniques and methods to carry out internal reconnaissance. This includes the enumeration of systems, hostss, processes, the execution of various commands to find out information such as the local user context and system configuration, hostname, IP addresses, active remote systems and programs running on the target systems. Security professionals can monitor the activities of an adversary by checking for unusual commands executed in the Batch scripts and PowerShell (LOTL) and by using packet capturing tools.
      Use of PowerShell: POSH can be used by an adversary as a tool for automating data exfiltration and launching further attacks. Check PowerShell transcript logs or Windows Event Logs, user agent string and IP addresses
      Unspecified Proxy Activities: An adversary can create and configure multiple domains pointing to the same host, allowing to switch quickly between the domains to avoid detection. Security professionals can find unspecified domains by checking the data feeds that are generated by those domains. Find any malicious files downloaded and unsolicited communication with the outside network based on the domains
      Use of Command-Line Interface: On gaining access to the target system, ad adversary can make use of the CLI to interact with the target system, browse the files, read file content etc. Check logs for process IDs and malicious files downloaded from the Internet
      HTTP User Agent: In HTTP - based communication, the server identifies the connected HTTP client using the user agent field. An adversary modifies the content of the HTTP user agent field to communicate with the compromised system and to carry out further attacks
      Command and Control Server: Adversaries use command and control servers to communicate remotely with compromised system through an encrypted session. Using the channel an adversary can steal data, delete data and launch further attacks
      Use of DNS Tunneling: Adversaries use DNS tunneling to obfuscate malicious traffic in the legitimate traffic carried by common protocols used in the network. Using DNS tunneling, an adversary can also communicate with the command and control server, bypass security controls and perform data exfiltration. Security professionals can identify DNS tunneling by analyzing malicious DNS requests, DNS payload, unspecified domains and destination of DNS requests
      Use of Web Shell: Adversaries uses a web shell to manipulate the web server by creating a shell within a website; it allows an adversary to gain remote access to the functionalities of a server. Using a web shell, an adversary performs tasks such as data exfiltration, file transfers and file uploads. Security professionals cna identify the web shell running in the network by analyzing server access, error logs, suspicious strings that indicate encoding, user agent strings etc.
      Data Staging: After successful penetration into a target's network, the adversary uses data staging techniques to collect and combine as much data as possible. Security professionals can detect data staging by monitoring network traffic for malicious file transfers, file integrity monitoring and event logs

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)

IoCs are the clues, artifacts and pieces of forensic data that are found on a network or OS of an organization that indicate a potential intrusion or malicious activity. IoCs are not intelligence, but rather a good source of information about threats that serves as data points in the intelligence process. Cybersecurity professionals use various automated tools to monitor IoCs to detect and prevent various security breaches.

      Atomic indicator -> those indicators that can't be segmented into smaller parts and whose meaning is not changed in the context of an intrusion (IP addresses, email addresses)
      Computed indicator -> obtained from data extracted from a security incident (hash values, regex values)
      Behavioral indicator -> grouping of both atomic and computed indicators, combined on the basis of some logic

IoCs is information regarding suspicious or malicious activities collected from various security establishments in a network's infrastructure.

Categories of IoC

The understanding of IoCs helps security professionals quickly detect the threats entering an organization and protect the organization from evolving threats

      Email Indicators
      Network Indicators
      Host based Indicators
      Behavioral Indicators

Email Indicators

Attackers usually prefer email services to send malicious data to the target organization or individual; socially engineered emails are preferred due to their ease of use and anonymity. Email Indicators are sender's email adress, email subject, attachment or hyperlinks

Network Indicators

Network Indicators are useful for command and control, malware delivery and identifying details about the OS, browser type and other computer specific info. Network Indicators: URLs, domain names, IP addresses

Host based Indicators

Host based Indicators are found by performing an analysis of the infected system within the organizational network. Host based Indicators: filenames, file hashes, registry keys, DLLs, mutex

Behavioral Indicators

Behavioral indicators are used to identify specific behavior related to malicious activities such as code injection into the memory or running scripts of an application. Behavioral Indicators: document executing PowerShell script, remote command execution

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

While the attack life cycle does a good job of describing the process an attacker goes through, it does not describe the specific behaviors used by the attacker, which are called techniques, tactics and procedures (TTP). The MITRE ATT&CK Framework is a taxonomy of TTPs, which means it is a way of organizing TTPs that have been seen in the real world into a set of categories. MITRE ATT&CK is a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. The knowledge base is used as a foundation for the development of specific threat models and methodologies in the private sector, government and in the cybersecurity product and service community.

Following are the stages the ATT&CK Framework identifies:

      Reconnaissance -> attacker is looking for victims or ways to get into victim's systems that have been identified
      Resource Development -> infrastructure for managing compromised hosts is put together here, as well as developing exploits or collecting credentials from other sources that could be used
      Initial Access -> systems or user accounts are compromised to provide the attacker access to a resource that can be used
      Execution -> not a stage, but instead describes a series of actions or behaviors an attacker might use to maintain access to a system; this could include execute PowerShell scripts (LOTL)
      Persistence -> the attacker needs to ensure they maintain access beyond reboots or other system changes, so they need to be sure they have a program that always runs when the system is started, or at least when a user logs in
      Privilege Escalation -> user behavior is restricted, attackers would typically look to gain administrative privileges
      Defense Evasion -> businesses do a lot to protect systems, looking for malware and instances of persistence; when attackers try to get access and maintain access regardless of the protection measures in place, it's defense evasion and may include masquerading or execution hijacking or tampering with protections in place
      Credential Access -> gathering usernames and password, done either from previous attacks or from systems or users directly; any set of username and password may be useful at some point
      Discovery -> any activity that collects information within the victim environment could be considered discovery
      Lateral Movement -> attackers will generally move from one system to another within the victim environment, to collect more information or to gather details about systems or users that could be used elsewhere
      Collection -> once the attacker has found information they want to use or sell, they need to pull it together; staging the data
      Command and Control -> the attacker needs a way of getting remote access to systems or to send commands to those systems; usually, there is infrastructure in place to perform this command and control work; because of firewalls direct access to victim systems is not possible so the connection needs to be initiated from the inside of the network
      Exfiltration -> collected data needs to be moved out to the attacker locations so it can be dealt with; moving the data out of the target environment to the attacker's place is exfiltration
      Impact -> attackers are not always looking to steal information; sometimes they are destructive or they modify data by encryption so victim's can't get access (ransomware)

The MITRE ATT&CK Framework continues to be updated with new TTPs as they are discovered

Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis

The Diamond Model, developed by expert analysts, introduces state-of-the-art technology for intrusion analysis. This model offers a framework and a set of procedures for recognizing clusters of events that are correlated on any of the systems in an organization. With the Diamond Model, more advanced and efficient mitigation approaches can be developed and analytic efficiency can be increased. The Diamond Model consists of four basic features:

      Adversary
      Capability
      Infrastructure
      Victim

Adversary

Refers to opponent or hacker responsible for the attack event. An adversary takes advantage of a capability against the victim to perform a malicious activity for financial benefit or damage. Adversaries can be insiders or competitor organizations.

Victim

The victim is the target that has been exploited or the environment where the attack was performed. The adversary exploits the vulnerabilities or security loopholes in the victim's infrastructure by using their resources. The victim can be any person, organization, institution or even network information such as IP addresses, domain names, email addresses and sensitive personal information of an individual.

Capability

Refers to all the strategies, methods and procedures associated with an attack. It can also be a malware or tool used against the target. Capability includes simple and complex attack techniques such as brute forcing and ransomware attacks.

Infrastructure

Refers to hardware or software used in the network by the target that has a connection with the adversary. It refers to What the adversary has used to reach the victim.

Information Security Controls

Information security controls prevent the occurence of unwanted events and reduce risk to the organization's information assets. It are the buttons and knobs, the controls, to turn in order to finetune the risks. The basic security concepts critical to information on the internet are:

      confidentiality
      integrity
      availability

The concepts related to the persons accessing the information are:

      authentication
      authorization
      non-repudiation

Information Assurance (IA)

IA refers to confidentiality, integrity, availability and authenticity of information and information systems during the usage, processing, storage and transmission of information. Information assurance is accomplished with phyical, technical and administrative controls:

      Develop local policy, process and guidance
      Design network and user authentication strategy
      Identify network vulnerabilities and threats
      Identify problems and resource requirements
      Apply appropriate information assurance controls
      Perform Certification and Accreditation (C&A) process of information systems to trace vulnerabilities and implement safety measures
      Provide information assurance training to all personnel

Continual / Adaptive Security Strategy

The adaptive security strategy prescribes that continuous prediction, prevention, detection and response actions must be taken:

      Protection: security policies, physical security, host security, firewall, IDS etc.
      Detection: network monitoring, packet sniffing tools
      Responding: respond to incidents and find their root causes, decide whether the incident is an actual security incident or a false positive
      Prediction: conduct risk and vulnerability assessment, perform attack surface analysis, consume threat intelligence data

Defense-in-Depth

Security strategy using several protection layers throughout an information system. This stragy uses the military principle that it is more difficult for an enemy to defeat a complex and multi-layered defense system than to penetrate a single barrier. Defense-in-Depth helps to prevent direct attacks against an information system and its data because a break in one layer leads the attacker to the next layer. Defense-in-Depth minimizes any adverse impact and gives administrators and engineers time to deploy new or updated countermeasures.

Risk

Risk refers to the degree of uncertainty or expectation of potential damage that an adverse event may cause to the system or its resources, under specified conditions. Alternatively, risk can also be:

      Probability of the occurence of a threat or an event that will damage, cause loss to, or have other negative impacts on the organization, either from internal or external liabilities
      Possibility of a threat acting upon an internal or external vulnerability and causing harm to a resource
      Product of the likelihood that an event will occur and the impact that the event might have on an information technology asset

The relation between Risk, Threats, Vulnerabilities and Impact:

RISK = Threats x Vulnerabilities x Impact

IT risk can be expanded to:

RISK = Threat x Vulnerability x Asset Value

Risk Level

Risk level is an assessment of the resulted impact on the network. Various methods exist. One of the common methods used to classify risks is to develop a two-dimensional matrix

Level of Risk = Consequence x Likelihood

There are four risk levels:

      extreme
      high
      medium
      low

Risk Matrix

The risk matrix scales the risk occurrence or likelihood probability, along with its consequences or impact. It is one of the simplest processes to use for increased visibility of risk and contributes to decision making. Although there are many standard risk matrices, individual organizations must create their own based on their business needs.

Risk Management

Risk Management is the process of identifying, assessing, responding to and implementing the activities that control how the organization manages the potential effects of risk. Objectives:

      Identify potential risks
      Identify impact of risks
      Prioritize the risks
      Understand and analyze the risks and report identified risk events
      Control the risk and mitigate its effect
      Create awareness among the security staff and develop strategies and plans for lasting risk management strategies

Risk management is a continuous process performed by achieving goals at every phase and is applied in all stages of the organization.

The four key steps commonly termed as risk management phases:

      Risk Identification -> uses skill set of people
      Risk Assessment
      Risk Treatment -> selecting and implementing appropriate controls
      Risk Tracking and Review

Cyber Threat Intelligence

Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) is the collection and analysis of information about threats and adversaries and the drawing up of patterns that provide an ability to make knowledgeable decisions for preparedness, prevention and response actions against various cyberattacks. The main aim of CTI is to make the organization aware of existing or emerging threats and prepare them to develop a proactive cybersecurity posture in advance of exploitation. Threat intelligence is useful in achieving secure data sharing and global transactions among organizations.

Types of Threat Intelligence:

      Strategic Threat Intelligence -> high level executives, CISO etc.
      Tactical Threat Intelligence -> cybersecurity professionals, security operations staff, architects etc.
      Operational Threat Intelligence -> security managers, heads of incident response, security forensics etc.
      Technical Threat Intelligence -> shorter lifespan, collected from active campaigns, updating IDS, IPS, Firewall etc.

Threat Intelligence Lifecycle

The threat intelligence lifecycle is a continuous process of developing intelligence from raw data. It can be compared to the OSINT lifecycle and has five phases:

      Planning and direction -> collection plan
      Collection -> OSINT, HUMINT, IMINT, MASINT, SIGINT, IoCs
      Processing and exploitation -> converting raw data into usable format
      Analysis and production -> finding facts, findings and forecasts; objective, timely, accurate and actionable
      Dissemination and integration -> distribution to intended consumers

      Strategic threat intelligence -> high-level executives and focus on high-level business strategies
      Operational threat intelligence -> cybersecurity specialists and focus on specific threats to the organization
      Tactical threat intelligence -> SOC managers, architects and focus on adversary TTPs
      Technical threat intelligence -> SOC staff and IR teams and focus on identified IoCs

Threat Modeling

Threat Modeling is a risk assessment approach for analyzing the security of an application and has three building blocks:

      Understanding the adversary's perspective
      Characterizing the security of the system
      Determining threats

Every application should have a developed and documented threat model that should be revisited as the application evolves. Focus on the approach and try not to be rigid. Start with a whiteboard.

The threat modeling process involves five steps:

      Identify Security Objectives -> what data should be protected, compliance requirements, QoS requirements etc.
      Application overview -> identify components, data flows, trust boundaries, use a whiteboard; Identify rols of users
      Decompose the application -> break it down to identify trust boundaries, data flows etc.
      Identify Threats
      Identify Vulnerabilities

Incident Management

Incident management is a set of defined processes to identify, analyze, prioritize and resolve security incidents to restore the system to normal service a.s.a.p.

      Vulnerability analysis
      Artifact analysis
      Security awareness training
      Intrusion detection
      Public or technology monitoring
      Conduct training sessions

Incident handling and response

IH&R is the process of taking organized and careful steps when reacting to a security incident or cyberattack. It is a set of procedures, actions and measures taken against an unexpected even occurence. It is a focused approach for restoring normal business operations as quickly as possible after an incident and with a minimal impact on the business.

      Step 1: Preparation -> audit of resources and assets to determine the purpose of security and define rules, policies and procedures that drive the IH&R process
      Step 2: Incident recording and assignment -> initial reporting and recording of the incident
      Step 3: Incident Triage -> security incidents are analyzed, validated, categorized and prioritized
      Step 4: Notification -> IH&R team informs various stakeholders, incl. management, third-party vendors, clients etc.
      Step 5: Containment -> prevent spread of infection to other organizational assets, preventing additional damage
      Step 6: Evidence gathering and forensic analysis -> accumulation of all possible evidence related to the incident and submitted to the forensic department for investigation
      Step 7: Eradication -> removal or elimination of the root cause
      Step 8: Recovery -> restoring affected system, services, resources and data through recovery
      Step 9: Post-Incident activities -> incident documentation, incident impact assessment, reviewing and revising policies, closing the investigation, incident disclosure

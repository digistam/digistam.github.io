Social Engineering

Social Engineering Concepts

Social engineering is the art of manipulating people to divulge sensitive information to use it to perform some malicious action. It targets the weakness of people. Attackers take a special interest in developing social engineering skills and can be so proficient that the victims might not even notice the fraud. Attackers ensure they know the organization's perimeter and the people on its perimeter such as security guards, receptionists, help-desk workers etc.

Human-based Social Engineering

      Impersonation -> attacker pretends to be legitimate or authorized person
      Vishing -> (voice or VoIP phishing) impersonation using voice to trick individuals, using Caller ID spoofing, pre-recorded messages and instructions resembling a legitimate financial institution
      Eavesdropping -> listening to a conversation or reading others' messages
      Shoulder surfing -> looking over someone's shoulder as the key information into a device
      Dumpster diving -> retrieving sensitive information by searching through trash bins
      Reverse Social Engineering -> perpetrator assumes the role of a knowledgeable professional so that the organization's employees ask them for information, the attacker manipulates questions to draw out the required information
      Piggybacking -> entry into a building or area with consent of the authorized person (hey, I forgot my ID badge, please let me in!)
      Tailgating -> entry into a building or area without consent of the authorized persion (wearing a fake badge, closey following an authorized person through a door that requires key access)
      Diversion Theft -> delivery professionals or transport companies deliver consignement to a wrong location where the goods are stolen
      Honey Trap -> attractive person begins a fake relationship with the victim
      Baiting -> attackers offer something alluring in exchange for information (also: usb flash drive dropped on the floor)
      Quid Pro Quo -> something for something, attackers keep calling random numbers claiming to be calling from technical support, baiting technique
      Elicitation -> extracting specific information from the victim by involving him in normal and disarming conversations, using good social skills

Computer-based Social Engineering

      Phishing -> the attacker registers a fake domain name, builds a lookalike website and mails the website link to users
      Spear Phising -> specialized social engineering content directed at specific employee(s)
      Whaling -> phishing high profile executives like CEO, CFO, politicians, celebrities
      Pharming -> redirecting urls to attacker controlled websites using DNS cache poisoning and host file modification, phishing without a lure
      Spimming -> spam over instant messaging
      Clone Phishing -> attacker creates a replica (clone) of a legit e-mail, website or other digital content; attacker creates nearly identical copy which contains a link or attachment and may have been previously received in its original form. Then, the attacker modifies certain elements, typically the link or attachment, to point to a malicious destination. If the victim falls for the cloned email and clicks on the link or opens the attachment, they are redirected to the malicious website
      E-wallet phishing -> target users of electronic wallets, aiming them to reveal login credentials, personal information or financial details; Initiallyl, the attacker sends a phishing email or messages to potential victims, posing as legit e-wallet providers. Once users land on the fake website, they are prompted to enter their login credentials, personal information or financial details, which the attackers capture in real time
      Tabnabbing and Reverse Tabnabbing -> social engineering attacks that target web users who have multiple tabs open in their web browser. These attacks exploit the user's tendency to switch between tabs without always checking the content of each tab. In a tabnabbing attack, a malicious actor crafts a webpage with code that detects when the user switches to another tab. While the user is focused on another tab, the malicious webpage dynamically changes its content to mimic a legitimate website that the user might have opened in one of their other tabs, such as a login page or a banking website. The user while returning to the tab containing the malicious webpage may be deceived into entering their login credentials or sensitive information, thinking they are interacting with the legitimate website. The attacker then captures the entered credentials or information, potentially leading to account takeover, identity theft or other malicious activites
      Reverse tabnabbing attack -> the attacker hosts a legit website and tricks users into clicking on a link that opens a new tab. The malicious link contains code that changes the content of the original tab to a phishing page or another malicious website. The user may not notice this change when they return to the original tab and then unknowingly enters their credentials or sensitive information, believing they are interacting with the legit website. This technique is particularly effective because users are more likely to trust content in tabs that they themselves opened
      Consent Phishing -> attacks the OAuth authentication protocol used by a.o. Google, Facebook and Microsoft. The attacker develops a legit looking fake application or website and lures the victim to visit it through various means, such as phishing emails, social media posts or online ads. Once the victim is on the site, the attacker uses persuasive language or misleading tactics to prompt the victim to grant permissions to access their account. Upon granting consent, the attacker gains access to the victim's account without actually having credential access and obtains information, including email addresses, contacts, profile data etc. and can even send spam emails or access other linked accounts
      Search Enging Phishing -> involves attackers manipulating search engine results, leading users to visit fraudulent websites crafted to steal sensitive information or distribute malware. Attackers use various techniques such as search engine optimization (SEO) manipulation, keyword stuffing etc. Fraudulent websites employ various tactics, incl. fake login pages, counterfeit forms, misleading content to trick users into revealing their login credentials, personal information or financial details, or unknowingly downloading malware
      Angler Phishing -> targetting disgruntled users or customers over sociale media platforms
      Catfishing -> identity theft, creating a fake profile and masquerade as the owner of the account, asking for money
      Deepfake attack -> phishing using deepfake, blackmail etc.

Crafting Phishing Emails with ChatGPT

Attackers can leverage AI - powered technologies to enhance social engineering tasks. With the aid of AI, attackers can effortlessly create and craft phishing emails to perform social engineering. Crafting phishing emails using AI can be a deceptive, yet effective, strategy in cyber fraud. Leveraging the language generation capabilities of AI, individuals with malicious intent can create convincing emails designed to trick recipients into divulging sensitive information or performing harmful actions. Using AI, attackers can also create convincing phishing emails designed to trick employees into downloading malware. By mimicking the language and style of legitimate communications, these emails can appear authentic and bypass traditional security measures. They often employ urgent requests or enticing offers to manipulate recipients into clicking malicious links or opening infected attachments, thus compromising the organization's cybersecurity defenses. Vigilance and employee training are crucial in combating such threats.

Example

Pose as a genuine Microsoft customer support executive with imaginary name; write a concise mail stating that he/she has found suspicious login on user's account and ask them to reset the password on urgent basis. Provide the reset link at [Fake Reset Link]i

The advantage of using ChatGPT for crafting phishing emails lie in its ability to generate diverse and contextually relevant content swiftly, reducing the time and effort required for manual composition. Additionaly, the dynamic nature of the model allows for the customization of emails based on evovling trends and recipient responses, enhancing the effectiveness of the phishing campaign. However, it is essential to recognize the ethical implications and potential legal consequences of using AI for malicious purposes, underscoring the importance of responsible AI usage and cybersecurity awareness.

Impersonation on Social Networking Sites

Social Engineering through impersonation on social networking sites

Two ways impersonation occurs:

      Creating fictitious profile of the victim
      Stealing victim's password or indirectly gaining access to the victim's social media account

Impersonation on Facebook

      Create a fake user group on Facebook identified as "Employees of" the target company
      Using a false identity, proceed to "friend" or invite actual employees
      Users join the group and provide their credentials such as date of birth, educational and employment backgrounds etc.
      Using the details of any one of the employees, an attacker can compromise a secured facility to gain access to the building

The Social-Engineer Toolkit (SET) is an open source Python driven tool aimed at penetration testing via social engineering. It is freely available and can be used to carry out a range of attacks. It allows attackers to draft email messages, attach malicious files and send them to a large number of people using spear phising. SET's multi-attack method allows Java applets, the Metasploit browser, Credential Harvester/Tabnabbing to be used simultaneously.

. It serves as a comprehensive toolbox for professionals to understand, simulate, and test the vulnerabilities associated with social engineering. SET offers an extensive range of tools and functionalities tailored to craft and deploy diverse social engineering attacks. These attacks encompass phishing campaigns, credential harvesting, the creation of malicious websites, and the generation of exploitative payloads, all engineered to exploit human psychology rather than solely relying on technical vulnerabilities.
